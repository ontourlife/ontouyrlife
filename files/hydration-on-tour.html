HYDRATION ON TOUR: THE ONE HABIT THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING

Between physical load-ins, loud environments, and coffee as a food group, most touring pros are chronically dehydrated. Here's how to actually fix it.

OTL

·
March 24, 2026
·
5 min read

Ask a touring professional what they drink on a show day and the answer is almost always some version of: coffee, energy drink, coffee, whatever's at catering, maybe a beer after the show. Water appears somewhere in that list for most people, but it's usually an afterthought rather than a foundation.

The result — which most people don't connect directly to their hydration — is a low-grade, chronic dehydration that persists for weeks or months at a time. Not the dramatic kind where you're dizzy and your mouth is dry. The quiet kind. The kind that looks like fatigue, brain fog, headaches that you attribute to the noise level, irritability that you attribute to the schedule, and a general feeling of operating slightly below full capacity that you've normalized as just what touring feels like.

It doesn't have to feel like that. And the fix is genuinely simple.

WHY TOURING DEHYDRATES YOU MORE THAN YOU REALIZE

The conditions of a show day stack dehydration risks in ways that aren't obvious until you think about them together. Venues are loud, which means your body is working harder to process noise. They're often hot, especially during load-in and show time. You're physically active — lifting, moving, standing for hours. You're drinking caffeine, which is mildly diuretic. You're often in air-conditioned buses and hotels overnight, which dry out the air. And you're almost certainly not drinking as much water as you think you are, because drinking water requires stopping, and stopping requires a moment that touring rarely provides.

By the time you're thirsty on a show day, you're already meaningfully dehydrated. Thirst is a late signal, not an early one. The headache that hits at hour eight isn't bad luck — it's your body sending a message it tried to send three hours ago through subtler means that you missed.

"By the time you're thirsty on a show day, you're already meaningfully dehydrated."

WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS WHEN YOU'RE DEHYDRATED

Even mild dehydration — as little as one to two percent of your body weight in fluid loss — measurably affects cognitive performance, mood, and physical energy. Studies consistently show impaired concentration, slower reaction time, increased perception of effort, and elevated cortisol levels in mildly dehydrated people doing tasks that should be routine.

For someone mixing front of house, operating lighting, managing a tour schedule, or doing any of the hundred other jobs that require sustained focus and fast decision-making, that cognitive hit is real and consequential. You're not performing at your best. The work feels harder than it should. Your patience shortens. Your creativity narrows. And because you feel this way every day on tour, you've stopped recognizing it as anything other than baseline.

The simplest performance upgrade available to most touring professionals is drinking significantly more water. Not a supplement, not a protocol, not a new routine. Just water, consistently, throughout the day.

HOW TO ACTUALLY DO IT

Knowing you should drink more water and actually drinking more water are different problems. The knowledge part is easy. The execution part on a show day — when you're constantly moving, constantly needed, and the water isn't always right in front of you — requires a bit more structure.

Get a large bottle and keep it visible. This sounds obvious and it is. The single biggest driver of water intake is whether or not water is immediately accessible. A 32oz or 1-litre bottle that you carry with you — not leave at the production desk, not keep in your bag — dramatically increases how much you drink simply by being in your field of vision. Out of sight, out of mind is completely real with hydration.

Drink a full glass immediately when you wake up. Before the coffee, before the phone, before anything else. Your body has been fasting and not drinking for however many hours you were asleep, and starting the day with water before caffeine sets a different baseline for the whole day. It takes thirty seconds and the cumulative effect over a tour is significant.

Tie water to existing habits. Every time you get a coffee, drink a glass of water first. Every time you check your phone, take a sip. Every time you walk past a water station at the venue, refill. You're not creating new habits — you're piggybacking on ones that already exist. This is consistently the most effective approach for people who struggle with the "I just forget" problem.

Watch the coffee-to-water ratio. Coffee isn't the enemy — most touring people would laugh at the suggestion of giving it up, and rightly so. But for every coffee, your body needs additional water to compensate. A rough rule: match each caffeinated drink with at least an equal volume of water. Two coffees means an extra 16oz of water, minimum.

ELECTROLYTES — WHEN WATER ALONE ISN'T ENOUGH

On high-sweat days — outdoor festivals, hot venues, heavy load-ins in summer — water alone isn't always sufficient. You're losing sodium, potassium, and magnesium alongside the fluid, and replacing the fluid without replacing the electrolytes can actually make you feel worse rather than better.

This doesn't mean you need to be buying expensive sports drinks all tour. A small amount of sodium in your water — a pinch of sea salt, or a low-sugar electrolyte tablet — is enough to make a meaningful difference on the heavy days. Coconut water, a banana, or a small amount of sports drink alongside regular water covers the same ground.

You'll know you need electrolytes rather than just water if you're drinking consistently and still feeling fatigued, cramping, or getting headaches. Those are signals that it's not just the volume — it's the composition.

Start tomorrow: bottle of water before the first coffee. Keep a large bottle visible all day. That's the whole protocol. Give it two weeks and notice what changes.

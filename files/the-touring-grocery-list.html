THE TOURING GROCERY LIST: WHAT TO BUY AT EVERY CITY STOP

Ten items every touring professional should grab when they have an hour and a grocery store nearby. Your catering backup plan, your energy insurance.

OTL
On Tour Life Team
Nutrition on the Road
·
April 4, 2026
·
4 min read

The grocery store near the hotel is one of the most valuable resources in any city and most touring people walk past it. They have thirty minutes before lobby call, they know there's catering at the venue, and a detour feels like an unnecessary complication in an already complicated day.

Here's the thing: the thirty minutes you spend in a grocery store at the start of a run pays back in energy, consistency, and control every single day that follows. The items on this list cost less than two meals at a venue bar and serve you across an entire leg of a tour.

This isn't a meal plan. It's a toolkit — the ten things that give you options when catering is bad, when schedules go sideways, when you need something at 2AM and the only other option is a vending machine.

THE LIST

1. Nut butter (individual packets or a small jar). Protein, fat, calories, shelf-stable. Goes with fruit, goes with bread, goes on a spoon at midnight. Single-serve packets travel better and don't require refrigeration after opening. This is the highest-utility item on the list.

2. A bag of mixed nuts. Same reasoning as nut butter but in snackable form. Almonds, cashews, walnuts — whatever you prefer. High satiety, long shelf life, no refrigeration. The touring snack that actually keeps you full between catering windows.

3. A large water bottle. If you don't already travel with one, buy one in the first city and keep it for the tour. The difference in hydration between having a bottle you carry versus relying on cups and cans is significant across a long run.

4. Electrolyte tablets or powder. Low weight, high value. Drop one in your water on heavy show days, outdoor festivals, or any stretch where you're sweating a lot. The difference between hydrated and hydrated-with-electrolytes on a physical day is noticeable.

5. Fruit that travels. Apples, bananas, oranges. Whole fruit that doesn't require refrigeration, doesn't bruise easily, and gives you actual nutrients rather than just calories. Buy enough for two to three days.

6. Oat packets (instant). Most hotel rooms have a kettle. Instant oats are a real breakfast that you control — with your nut butter, with some of the fruit, maybe with honey from the café downstairs. Costs pennies, takes three minutes, and is infinitely better than whatever was at the continental breakfast.

7. A good protein bar (not a candy bar). There are protein bars that are effectively candy bars with extra protein powder. Buy the ones that aren't. Look for something with 15g+ protein, less than 10g sugar, and ingredients you recognize. Buy four or five. They live in your bag and exist for the moments when there is genuinely nothing else.

8. Dark chocolate. This is a morale item as much as a nutrition item, and morale matters on a long tour. A good bar of 70%+ dark chocolate provides magnesium, is genuinely satisfying, and has enough sugar to be a useful pick-me-up without the spike-crash of milk chocolate. Buy one. Make it last a few days. Enjoy it.

9. Herbal tea bags. Especially if anxiety or sleep is an issue. Chamomile, valerian, passionflower — these aren't magic, but they're a genuine wind-down tool that most hotels can support with a kettle and hot water. A small box costs almost nothing and lasts weeks.

10. Something local. One item that is specific to the city you're in — a local bread, a regional product, something from the deli counter you've never tried. This sounds trivial and it is, slightly. But across a long tour, making one small intentional choice per city keeps you present in the places you're passing through rather than just rolling through them on autopilot.

THE HABIT

The goal isn't to do a full grocery shop in every city. It's to develop the habit of a twenty-minute stop when the opportunity presents itself — enough to restock the essentials, enough to have options, enough to not be entirely dependent on whatever the production has sorted for the day.

The touring people who eat well consistently aren't the ones with the best catering. They're the ones who treat their own nutrition as a responsibility they take on themselves rather than something that happens to them.

Screenshot this list. Next city stop with thirty minutes free — use it. Your energy levels for the next three days will thank you.

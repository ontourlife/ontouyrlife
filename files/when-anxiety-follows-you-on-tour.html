WHEN ANXIETY FOLLOWS YOU ON TOUR

The hum of anxiety that doesn't stop when the show does — and what to do when the road amplifies it.

OTL
On Tour Life Team
Mental Health
·
April 18, 2026
·
7 min read

Anxiety on tour doesn't look like the anxiety in the diagrams. It doesn't always announce itself. It doesn't always look like panic or avoidance or the obvious things people associate with the word. On the road, it often looks like something else entirely.

It looks like the grip you feel before every load-in, even after hundreds of them. The rehearsal of everything that could go wrong that runs automatically in the back of your mind while you're doing the work. The way you check the same connection three times even though you already know it's solid. The difficulty unwinding after a show that went perfectly, because some part of you is already preparing for what comes next.

It looks normal, in the context of touring. And that's exactly the problem.

WHY TOURING CREATES A PERFECT ANXIETY ENVIRONMENT

Anxiety thrives under specific conditions: uncertainty, high stakes, lack of control, and sustained pressure over time. Touring provides all four in abundance.

The uncertainty is structural. You never fully know what you're walking into. New venue, new crew, new variables, new problems. The anticipatory mind — the part of your brain that tries to stay one step ahead of problems — is working overtime on every tour day, because there is always something that needs anticipating.

The high stakes are real. In a live show environment, mistakes happen in front of thousands of people with no way to undo them. The weight of that reality sits somewhere in your nervous system even when the show is going well. Especially when the show is going well, because something going well means something could stop going well.

The lack of control is chronic. Your schedule is someone else's. Your location is someone else's. Your food, your sleeping situation, your daily structure — all of it determined by forces you have minimal input into. For an anxiety-prone nervous system, that sustained lack of control is genuinely taxing in ways that compound over a long tour.

"Anxiety on the road doesn't announce itself. It often looks like professionalism."

THE POINT WHERE USEFUL BECOMES HARMFUL

Not everything that looks like anxiety is a problem. A certain level of vigilance, preparation, and anticipatory thinking is part of what makes a great touring professional. The person who checks twice is often the person who catches the problem nobody else saw. The person who thinks through contingencies before they're needed is the person the team relies on when something goes sideways.

The question isn't whether you're anxious. It's whether the anxiety is serving you or costing you more than it's worth.

Serving you looks like: heightened focus during critical moments, thorough preparation that prevents actual problems, alertness that keeps you responsive in fast-moving situations.

Costing you looks like: inability to rest even when rest is available, intrusive thoughts during off-time, physical symptoms (tight chest, shallow breathing, tense shoulders) that persist outside work hours, difficulty being present in non-work moments, an escalating sense of dread that isn't proportional to actual risk.

Most touring people who are dealing with anxiety at the harmful end of that spectrum have been managing it privately for years — normalizing it as the cost of caring about the work, or mistaking it for professionalism, or simply not having the vocabulary to describe what's happening.

WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS ON THE ROAD

The pre-show ritual isn't just superstition. Most experienced touring people have some version of a pre-show routine — a sequence of actions they do in a specific order before things kick off. Part of the function of that ritual is reducing uncertainty by creating a small pocket of predictability. If you don't have one, building one is genuinely useful for anxiety management. The routine doesn't have to be complicated. It just has to be consistent.

Name the specific worry, don't sit in general dread. Anxiety is often most powerful when it's vague. The general sense that something could go wrong is harder to manage than the specific thought: "I'm worried about the RF situation in this venue." Specific worries can be addressed, checked, or consciously set aside. Vague dread just circulates. When you notice the hum of anxiety, the question worth asking is: what exactly am I worried about? Often the answer makes the anxiety smaller, not larger.

Physical movement breaks the anxiety loop. Anxiety lives in the body as much as the mind — tight breathing, tension in the shoulders and chest, elevated heart rate. Physical movement, even brief, interrupts the physiological state that sustains anxious thinking. A five-minute walk before a difficult load-in, some shoulder rolls before the doors open, anything that asks your body to do something different from bracing — these aren't trivial. They're interrupts that actually work.

The post-show decompression is non-negotiable if anxiety is high. The show creates a physiological state of high alert. Walking directly from show-mode to sleep-mode asks your nervous system to do something it isn't built to do quickly. A deliberate transition — even 30 minutes of something quiet and low-stimulation — gives your system permission to come down. Without it, the alert state continues into what should be rest, which compounds anxiety across the tour.

WHEN TO TALK TO SOMEONE

There's a threshold where anxiety management techniques aren't enough — where what you're dealing with has become something that genuinely limits your functioning or your quality of life, and where working with a mental health professional would make a real difference.

That threshold is closer than most people think. You don't have to be unable to work. You don't have to be in crisis. If the anxiety is affecting your sleep consistently, if it's affecting your relationships, if it's creating a background of suffering that you're carrying through every day of every tour — that's a reason to talk to someone, not a sign that you need to push harder or manage it better on your own.

If this landed somewhere real, that's information worth taking seriously. Anxiety that's been normalized for years doesn't have to stay that way. Speaking to a mental health professional is available to you wherever you are in the world — and it's easier to access than it used to be.

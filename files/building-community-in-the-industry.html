BUILDING A COMMUNITY IN AN INDUSTRY THAT MOVES CONSTANTLY

How to find your people in live events when everyone is always somewhere else — and how to maintain those connections across years of touring.

OTL
On Tour Life Team
Connection & Community
·
March 7, 2026
·
6 min read

The live events industry is, structurally, terrible at community. Everyone is always moving. Projects are finite. The people you work with on one tour scatter across different tours immediately after. There are no offices, no regular colleagues, no stable professional location that functions as a social anchor.

And yet the industry has some of the strongest community bonds of any professional world. The people who've been in it for twenty years often describe it as their primary social world, their deepest friendships, the community that understands them in ways nobody outside it quite does.

That community doesn't form by accident. It forms because certain people decide to build it deliberately, and over time other people gather around them.

WHY COMMUNITY IN THIS INDUSTRY IS DIFFERENT

The bonds that form in live events have a particular quality that comes from shared adversity and shared purpose. You work with someone through a difficult show, a technical crisis, a load-out in the rain at 2AM, a tour that went sideways in three different cities — and you know something about them that a normal colleague would take years to discover. You know how they function under pressure. You know whether they're someone you'd want in the foxhole. You know if they're real.

That accelerated intimacy is one of the gifts of the industry. The challenge is that it doesn't automatically create lasting community — it creates intense temporary connection that dissipates when the tour ends unless someone actively maintains it.

"The bonds that form in live events are built from shared adversity. The challenge is making them last beyond the tour."

THE PEOPLE WHO BUILD REAL COMMUNITIES

In almost every regional live events scene, there are certain people who function as community anchors — people around whom a genuine professional and social community has formed. They're rarely the most famous or the most technically skilled. They're the ones who do certain specific things consistently.

They introduce people to each other. When they meet someone worth knowing, they actively connect them to other people worth knowing. "You should meet X, you'd get along and you're working in similar spaces." Simple as that. The network effect of this, done consistently over years, is enormous.

They maintain contact across the gaps. They reach out between tours — not just when they need something, but when they think of someone, when they see something that reminds them of a person, when someone had a big show or a hard week. The contact costs almost nothing and it communicates that the relationship exists outside of work proximity.

They show up when it matters. Industry events, memorial shows, opening nights, farewell tours. The moments that are significant to people in the community. Being present at those moments — physically or through explicit acknowledgment — is what separates relationships that stay professional from ones that become genuine.

They talk about the real stuff. Mental health, career uncertainty, the hard parts of the lifestyle, the things that don't appear in the professional persona. By being willing to have those conversations, they create spaces where other people feel safe having them too. That's how professional networks become actual communities.

HOW TO FIND YOUR PEOPLE IF YOU'RE NEWER TO THE INDUSTRY

The community isn't immediately visible when you're new. It exists in the same spaces as the work but it's not announced. Finding it requires being present, being consistent, and being genuinely interested in the people around you rather than just the professional opportunities they represent.

Industry forums, online communities for specific disciplines, local networking events for live events professionals — these exist in most major markets and many smaller ones. They're not glamorous, but they're where the people who are serious about their career and their place in the community tend to show up. Going once is worth almost nothing. Going consistently over months starts to build something.

The single most reliable community-building act available to someone newer to the industry is being genuinely useful to the people around them — not in a transactional way, but in the way that makes people want to work with you again, refer you to others, include you in things. Community in live events is downstream of reputation, and reputation is built one show at a time.

The community is there. It's just not centralized or visible from the outside. Show up consistently, be genuinely useful, maintain the connections you make — and over time, you'll find yourself inside something real.

HANDLING CONFLICT ON TOUR WITHOUT BURNING BRIDGES

Tight quarters, high pressure, exhausted people. The conditions for conflict on the road are always present. Here's how to navigate it and come out with the relationships intact.

6 min read

Touring creates conflict. Not because the people involved are particularly difficult — though sometimes they are — but because the conditions are almost designed to produce friction. Exhaustion, close proximity, high stakes, limited personal space, hierarchical pressure, and the sustained removal from the normal anchors of life. Put any group of people in those conditions for long enough and conflict will appear.

How you handle it when it does is one of the defining professional skills of a touring career. Not because conflict-free people are more employable — they often just have conflict they're not addressing, which creates its own problems. But because the ability to navigate conflict without escalating it, without damaging relationships permanently, and without creating a drama that the rest of the crew has to absorb is genuinely rare and genuinely valued.

THE CONFLICT TYPES AND WHAT THEY NEED

Not all touring conflict is the same kind of thing, and treating it all the same way produces inconsistent results.

Operational conflict — disagreements about how something should be done, competing priorities, resource conflicts — is best handled quickly, directly, and factually. Strip the emotion out of it. "We have a conflict between X and Y — here's what I think we should do, here's why. What's your read?" Fast, clean, solution-focused. Most operational conflict resolves easily when it's separated from personal friction.

Interpersonal friction — accumulated small irritations, personality clashes, style differences — is harder because it doesn't have a clean factual resolution. The person who leaves gear in the wrong place, or communicates in a way that reads as dismissive, or has a working style that grates. This category benefits most from the direct but non-confrontational conversation — "I want to flag something small before it becomes a bigger thing" — had early, when there's still goodwill on both sides.

Serious professional conflict — safety violations, ethical issues, significant misconduct — is a different category entirely and needs to go through the appropriate channels rather than being handled peer-to-peer. Know the difference between something you can and should resolve directly and something that needs a tour manager or production manager involved.

THE CONVERSATION THAT PREVENTS MOST CONFLICTS

The majority of serious touring conflicts — the ones that become crew drama, that poison the atmosphere for weeks, that end in someone being replaced — started as something small that nobody addressed early enough. A frustration that was managed privately until it wasn't. A pattern that was tolerated until it became intolerable. A misunderstanding that calcified into a grievance.

The most conflict-resistant touring professionals are not the ones who avoid conflict — it's the ones who have small, direct, early conversations that prevent small issues from becoming large ones.

"Hey, I want to flag something — when X happens, it creates Y problem for me. Can we work out a different approach?" is an extraordinarily powerful sentence. It's specific, it's non-accusatory, it invites collaboration rather than creating defensiveness, and it addresses the issue before it has accumulated enough weight to make a clean conversation difficult.

Most people don't have this conversation because it feels confrontational. It isn't. Confrontation is what happens when you don't have it and the frustration eventually comes out sideways.

WHEN CONFLICT HAS ALREADY ESCALATED

When the conversation is no longer easy — when there's already heat in the relationship, when things have been said or not said and the tension is real — the approach needs to change.

Wait for the right moment. Conflict conversations held during load-in, immediately after a difficult show, or in front of an audience almost always make things worse. Find a neutral moment — catering, a quiet corridor, a time when neither person is under immediate pressure — and initiate there.

Separate the behaviour from the person. "When X happens, it creates Y problem" is more productive than any version of "you are X kind of person." The first addresses something that can be changed. The second just creates defensiveness.

Be willing to own your part. Even in conflicts where you believe the other person is primarily at fault, there is almost always something you contributed — a communication style that didn't land well, a assumption you made, something you said or didn't say. Acknowledging that openly, genuinely, disarms a significant amount of defensive energy and creates room for the other person to do the same.

Know when to involve a third party. Some conflicts between two people need a neutral third party to facilitate — not as a formal complaint, but as a tour manager or mutual colleague who can help both people feel heard and move toward a workable resolution. Asking for that help is not an escalation. It's a recognition that some conversations are genuinely hard to have without support.

The touring world is smaller than it looks. The people you work with this year are the people who will be staffing tours for the next decade. Handle conflict in a way you'll be proud of — not because it's nice, but because how you handle the hard moments is what people remember.

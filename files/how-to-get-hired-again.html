HOW TO GET HIRED AGAIN: WHAT TOUR MANAGERS ACTUALLY LOOK FOR

The real reasons people get called back — and the real reasons they don't. Straight talk about what actually matters when the next tour is being crewed.

OTL
On Tour Life Team
Professional Development
·
February 14, 2026
·
7 min read

The call to crew a tour doesn't come from a job board. It comes from a person who has worked with you before, or from someone who has worked with someone who has worked with you before. The pipeline is almost entirely relational, which means your professional reputation is not a nice-to-have — it is the mechanism by which your career actually functions.

Understanding what goes into that reputation — what tour managers and production managers are actually thinking about when they build their crew lists — is one of the highest-value things a touring professional can invest in understanding. Not to game the system, but because the things that lead to being called back are also the things that lead to a sustainable, meaningful career.

THE BASICS, WHICH AREN'T ACTUALLY BASIC

Show up on time. Do what you said you'd do. Don't create problems. These seem obvious but the number of experienced, technically skilled people who consistently fail at one or more of them is significant enough that the people who nail all three stand out immediately.

Reliability — genuine, consistent reliability — is the foundational professional currency in live touring. More than skill level. More than personality. More than connection. If a tour manager knows that when you say you'll handle something, it will be handled, and that you will be where you're supposed to be when you're supposed to be there — that is a profoundly valuable thing. The technical skill can be developed. The reliability either is or isn't there, and people learn very quickly which one you are.

"Reliability is the foundational professional currency in live touring. More than skill. More than personality."

WHAT ACTUALLY GETS YOU CALLED BACK

You were easy to have around. This sounds trivial and it is anything but. A six-week tour with difficult personalities is a genuinely bad six weeks for everyone on it. Tour managers and production managers carry the responsibility for crew culture and they know it. Someone who is competent, professional, positive without being irritating, and easy to have in close quarters for weeks on end is worth a significant premium over someone who is more skilled but more complicated.

You solved problems without being asked. The crew members who get called back most reliably are the ones who notice things and address them — not in ways that overstep their role, but in the small constant way of someone who is paying attention. The cable that was going to be a trip hazard. The rigging detail that needed a second look. The logistical conflict nobody had noticed yet. Proactive problem-solving is memorable. Reactive problem-solving is just doing the job.

You handled the hard moments well. Every tour has hard moments — the show that goes sideways, the venue that's a disaster, the day when everything that can go wrong does. How you perform in those moments is more remembered than how you perform when things are fine. Calm, focused, solution-oriented under pressure is the thing that gets specifically mentioned when people are recommending you to someone else.

You were good to the newer people. The way experienced crew members treat people earlier in their careers is noticed — by the newer people, obviously, but also by the people above them. Someone who shares knowledge generously, who brings up the level of the people around them rather than protecting their expertise, who treats everyone on the crew with the same professional respect regardless of their experience level — that person is building a reputation that propagates through the industry in ways that pay back for years.

WHAT DOESN'T GET YOU CALLED BACK

Complaining — about the venue, the schedule, the production, the artist, the catering. One or two moments of frustration are human and understandable. A pattern of complaint drains the crew around you and marks you as someone who adds difficulty to an already difficult job.

Being unreachable during key windows. Tour days have critical communication periods — pre-show, transitions, problem windows. Being the person who can't be found during those windows, or who takes too long to respond when time matters, is a real professional liability.

Making it about you. The show is not about you. The production is not about you. Your role is to serve the show. The people who struggle most in this industry long-term are the ones who never quite internalize that, and whose decisions are consistently filtered through their own visibility, credit, or comfort rather than the needs of the production.

Your reputation is being built on every show, every day, in every small interaction. It's not about doing anything extraordinary. It's about doing the ordinary things extraordinarily well, consistently, over time.

THE 10-MINUTE STRETCH ROUTINE EVERY CREW MEMBER NEEDS

Load-in wrecks your body in very specific ways. This routine targets exactly those ways — no mat required, no warm-up needed, fits in any hotel corridor.

·
4 min read

Load-in does very specific things to your body. It's not the same as a general workout or a day of walking. It's heavy lifting with rotation, repeated shoulder-level carries, sustained static holds while something is being bolted into place, a lot of bending at the waist without proper hip hinge, and a great deal of time on concrete with inadequate footwear.

The stiffness that accumulates across a tour isn't mysterious. It's predictable — and because it's predictable, it's largely preventable, or at least manageable, with ten minutes of targeted stretching done consistently.

This routine is designed for the specific physical demands of crew work. It targets the areas that loading and rigging and cable runs and extended venue time actually affects: the lower back, the hip flexors, the thoracic spine, the shoulders, and the hamstrings. It requires no equipment, no warm-up, and can be done in a hotel room, a dressing room corridor, or a corner of the loading dock while waiting for the truck.

THE ROUTINE — 10 MINUTES

1. Doorway chest opener — 60 seconds. Stand in a doorway, place both forearms on the frame at shoulder height, and gently lean forward until you feel a stretch across your chest and the front of your shoulders. This directly counteracts the forward-rounded posture that comes from hours of carrying and cable-running. Hold the stretch, breathe into it, let it open gradually.

2. Standing hip flexor stretch — 45 seconds each side. Step one foot forward into a lunge position, lower the back knee toward the floor, and push your hips forward gently. The hip flexors — the muscles connecting your lower back to your thighs — shorten dramatically from sustained standing and lifting. This is the most important stretch on the list for lower back pain prevention.

3. Seated thoracic rotation — 30 seconds each side. Sit on the edge of a chair or the bed. Cross your arms over your chest. Rotate your upper body slowly to each side as far as it comfortably goes. The thoracic spine (the mid-back) stiffens severely from repetitive lifting and forward bending. This rotation keeps it mobile and reduces the compensation patterns that lead to lower back injury.

4. Standing hamstring stretch — 45 seconds each side. Place one foot on a chair or box at roughly hip height. Keep both legs straight and hinge forward from the hips — not the waist — until you feel a stretch down the back of the elevated leg. Tight hamstrings are a direct contributor to lower back pain, and crew work tightens them continuously.

5. Shoulder cross-body stretch — 30 seconds each side. Bring one arm across your body at chest height and use the other arm to gently press it closer to your chest. Holds for overhead rigging work and carrying cases load the posterior shoulder, which rarely gets stretched in the opposite direction during the work day. This one reduces the accumulated tension that becomes shoulder pain by week three of a tour.

6. Cat-cow spinal mobilization — 60 seconds. On all fours (hands and knees), alternate between arching your back toward the ceiling (cat) and letting it sag toward the floor (cow), moving slowly and breathing with the movement. This mobilizes the entire spine and is one of the most reliable lower back relief exercises known. If you only do one thing from this list, make it this.

7. Child's pose — 60 seconds. From kneeling, sit back toward your heels and reach your arms forward on the floor, letting your forehead rest down. This decompresses the lower spine, stretches the hips, and provides a brief moment of complete physical release that touring work almost never otherwise provides. Hold it for a full minute. Breathe. Let the day go slightly.

WHEN TO DO IT

The best time is immediately after load-in, before the show work begins — while your muscles are warm from activity and the evening's demand is still ahead of you. The second best time is after load-out, before sleep. The third best time is any time at all.

Ten minutes. Every day of the tour if possible. Every other day as a minimum. The cumulative effect across a six-week run is the difference between arriving at the end of the tour feeling battered but functional, and arriving feeling like something that needs several weeks of physiotherapy before it resembles a person again.

Save this article or screenshot the routine. Do it tonight after load-out. Your lower back will still be a member of your body by week six.

COMMUNICATION THAT DOESN'T GET YOU FIRED: WORKING WITH ARTISTS, MANAGEMENT, AND PROMOTERS

The unspoken rules of professional communication in live events — what separates the people who keep getting called back from the ones who don't.

OTL
On Tour Life Team
Professional Development
·
April 7, 2026
·
6 min read

Nobody in this industry gets fired for being bad at their job. Not usually, anyway. The people who stop getting calls — the ones whose names quietly disappear from the shortlist — almost always lost their spot because of how they communicated, not because of what they could do.

This is one of the most important things to understand about a career in live touring: technical skill gets you in the room. Communication keeps you there. And the gap between people who are brilliant at their craft and people who are brilliant at their craft and consistently employed is, more often than not, a communication gap.

The rules aren't complicated. But they're also not obvious, and nobody teaches them explicitly. You're supposed to absorb them from watching people who already know — which means if you didn't start your career around the right people, you might have spent years not knowing what you were missing.

UNDERSTAND THE HIERARCHY — AND RESPECT IT WITHOUT BEING SERVILE

Touring has a clear hierarchy and everyone in it knows where they sit. That hierarchy exists for operational reasons — in a high-pressure live environment, decisions need to be made fast and by the right people. Understanding it isn't about ego or politics. It's about efficiency.

The mistake most people make isn't ignoring the hierarchy — it's either over-respecting it to the point of uselessness, or under-respecting it to the point of causing friction. Both are problems.

Over-respecting it looks like this: something is wrong, you can see it, you know the fix, but you don't say anything because it's "not your department" or you don't want to overstep. This makes you less valuable, not more professional. The people who get called back are the ones who solve problems, not the ones who wait to be asked.

Under-respecting it looks like bypassing your department head to go directly to the tour manager, or offering unsolicited opinions in the artist's presence, or contradicting someone senior in front of the client. Even if you're right, the way you communicated it costs more than being wrong would have.

The line between them is this: speak up about problems, but direct that communication through the right channels and at the right times.

"Technical skill gets you in the room. Communication keeps you there."

HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH ARTISTS

This is where a lot of crew members go wrong, especially earlier in their careers. Artists are your clients. They are also often anxious, tired, overstimulated, and managing a hundred things you can't see. How you interact with them — even briefly, even tangentially — leaves an impression that gets relayed back to management whether you know it or not.

Less is more. Your job is to solve problems, not to have conversations. If you need something from an artist, be specific, be brief, and be done. "I need thirty seconds for a sound check on your monitor mix" is a complete sentence. You don't need to explain why, apologize for the interruption, or fill the silence with small talk.

Never complain in their presence. Not about the venue, not about the schedule, not about another crew member, not about the tour. You are there to make things work. Voicing frustration in earshot of the artist or their immediate team creates an impression of instability that is very hard to walk back.

Read the room before approaching. There are moments in a show day when an artist is available and moments when they are absolutely not. Pre-show is almost always the latter. Learn which is which as fast as possible on any new tour, and build your communication timing around it.

HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH MANAGEMENT AND PROMOTERS

Management and promoters operate on a different clock than crew. They are managing relationships, money, and logistics across multiple moving parts simultaneously. What they need from you is reliability and clarity — not creativity, not opinions, not updates about things that are already handled.

Report problems early, not late. The single most valued communication habit in this industry is telling someone about a problem before it becomes a crisis. A tour manager who hears "I think we might have an issue with the rigging cert at the next venue" on Wednesday is a different conversation than the same sentence on Saturday morning. Early warning, even about things that turn out fine, builds trust. Late warning, even about things you fixed, erodes it.

Come with solutions, not just problems. When you do flag an issue, lead with what you think the solution is, even if you're not sure it's right. "We have a problem with X and I think we can fix it by doing Y — want me to proceed?" is infinitely more valuable than "we have a problem with X." The second one puts the cognitive load on someone else. The first one shows you're thinking.

Written confirmation is your friend. Verbal agreements in the chaos of a show day get forgotten, misremembered, or disputed. If something important has been decided — a change to the schedule, an approval for additional gear, a budget exception — follow it up with a brief message. Not to cover yourself legally, but to make sure everyone is actually working from the same information.

THE THING THAT SEPARATES VETERANS FROM EVERYONE ELSE

The most experienced people in this industry share one communication trait that is almost invisible until you know to look for it: they regulate their emotional state before they communicate.

When something goes wrong on a show day — and something always goes wrong — there's a window of about thirty seconds where the response sets the tone for everything that follows. Veterans use that window. They take a breath. They assess before they speak. They communicate what is happening and what they're doing about it in a tone that is calm and factual, regardless of what they're feeling internally.

That ability — to have a serious problem and communicate about it without transmitting panic, blame, or frustration — is worth more than almost any technical skill in live production. It's also entirely learnable. It just requires deciding, in advance, that it's how you want to show up.

Most communication problems on tour come down to timing, tone, and channel — saying the right thing, in the right way, to the right person. Get those three right and almost everything else takes care of itself.
